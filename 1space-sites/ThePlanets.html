Overview

The Ēnlū System hosts a diverse array of planets, each with unique characteristics shaped by their proximity to the star Ēnlū and their individual compositions. The inner region of the system, closest to Ēnlū, contains rocky terrestrial planets like Kle and Tleteuāts, with Tleteuāts being the only planet known to support life. These inner planets feature solid surfaces and, in some cases, atmospheres that interact dynamically with their environments.


Beyond the system's frost line, in the outer region, lie the gas giants Ūtsatsa, Chinālo, and Shunāta. These massive planets are composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with thick atmospheres that harbor turbulent weather patterns and powerful storms. Each gas giant is orbited by multiple moons, some of which present intriguing landscapes and potential for future exploration.

Inner Planets: Kle

Kle, nestled closest to its parent star, emerges as a rugged stone planet, enduring the scorching intensity of its proximity to the stellar furnace. Devoid of any substantial atmosphere, Kle's surface is exposed to the unrelenting barrage of solar radiation, resulting in extreme temperatures that fluctuate drastically depending on the planet's position relative to its star. With no protective atmosphere to moderate its climate, Kle experiences a temperature range that is exceptionally variable, with temperatures soaring to blistering highs during the day and plummeting to chilling lows at night. The planet's axial tilt of 25.0 degrees further exacerbates these temperature extremes, leading to a harsh and unforgiving environment across its barren surface.


Despite its inhospitable conditions, Kle remains a prominent feature in the night sky when viewed from the nearby celestial body Tleteuāts. With an apparent magnitude of -2.72, Kle shines brightly against the backdrop of space, serving as a stark reminder of the diverse and often hostile nature of the cosmos. However, due to its proximity to the star, Kle is too close to observe directly, its surface obscured by the intense glare of its parent star.


As one of the closest planets to its star, Kle offers a glimpse into the extreme conditions that can exist in the vicinity of stellar bodies, serving as a testament to the resilience of celestial bodies in the face of adversity.

Physical Characteristics:

Tleteuāts is best categorised as a “Earth-like”, due to its comparable but smaller mass, radius, density, and surface gravity. Its structure consists of an outermost silicate crust, a molten silicate mantle, a liquid iron outer core (whose flow generates the planet’s magnetic field) and a solid iron inner core. The planet’s surface is comprised of landmasses and liquid water oceans. The landmasses account for 24.98% of the total surface area and are broken up into two main continents. In order of decreasing area, they are: WEST CONTINENT AND EAST CONTINENT. The current layout of the continents, in conjunction with the planet’s axial tilt (see next section), gives rise to a moderate global climate with a diverse array of climate zones across the planet. Our simulations indicate that the landmasses last formed a supercontinent about 450 million years ago, and the models predict the next supercontinent on Tleteuāts will occur within the next few 100 million years.

Orbital Characteristics:

A day on the Tleteuāts is about 22.8 Earth hours long, with the star rising in the east and setting in the west. Temperate and polar regions on Tleteuāts experience moderate seasonal variations in heating attributed to the planet’s rotational axis being tilted approximately 7° with respect to the plane of the ecliptic. In the tropics (approximately 14° south to 14° north), the sun is directly overhead for at least one day per year, whereas in the polar regions (approximately 83° to 90° north and south), the sun never rises or sets for at least one day per year. Tleteuāts orbits its star at an average distance of 1.54 AU. Though this distance varies throughout the year, the planet remains at all times within the system’s habitable zone—a region of space stretching from 1.42 to 2.05 AU where temperatures are such that liquid water can exist on its surface.

Atmosphere:

Tleteuāts atmosphere consists of mostly nitrogen and oxygen, though notably, its carbon dioxide content is rather high. It is suspected that this is a result of the increased volcanism occurring as a result of WEST CONTINENT beginning to rift apart. The high levels of CO2 have raised the planet’s temperature such that it is within human tolerances. Atmospheric surface pressure and density are also rather low, which impacts the local flora and fauna. Tleteuāts atmosphere is divided into three circulation cells per hemisphere: a Hadley cell extends from the equator to 30° north and south, a Ferrel cell extends from 30° to 60° north and south, and a final polar cell extends from 60° north and south to the poles of the planet. At noon, when the star is at its zenith, the sky is a gray - bluish colour. Near sunrise and sunset, the sky turns a deep, dark grayish colour fading to warmer yellow and orange hues near the horizon.

Tleteuāts - Getsi System:

Tleteuāts is encircled by a lone natural satellite known as Getsi. This moon exerts gravitational forces that result in tidal variations on Tleteuāts, with spring high tides reaching approximately 8 meters and spring low tides dropping to around -8 meters. During neap tides, the high tide elevates by about 1 meter, while the low tide decreases to approximately -1 meter. Because of Getsi's angular size, it occasions both total and annular eclipses on Tleteuāts.

Plant life on Tleteuāts:

Plant life on Tleteuāts showcases extraordinary adaptations enabling efficient utilization of the star's light for photosynthesis. The majority of flora thriving on Tleteuāts have evolved to harness the optimal wavelengths emitted by the star, ensuring their sustainability and proliferation. This astute adaptation manifests in foliage displaying a rich spectrum of colors, ranging from a deep, luscious wine red to a velvety, enigmatic dark purple.

Formation:

It is thought that Getsi formed long ago as a result of a collision between Tleteuāts and another proto-planetary object.

Physical Characteristics:

Getsi is a substantial major satellite. Its mass, radius, density, and gravity are higher than those of any other moon in the Ēnlū system. It has a magnetic field and an atmosphere, though both are extremely tenuous. The Moon’s surface is marked by a moderate amount of impact craters, dormant shield volcanoes, and volcanic domes. It plays an important role in regulating Tleteuāts climate by stabilising its axial tilt.

Orbital and Rotational Characteristics:

Getsi orbits Tleteuāts at a mean distance of 176300 km, taking 16.76 local days to complete a full cycle (new moon to new moon), also known as a moon month. Getsi is tidally locked to Tleteuāts, therefore its rotational period about its axis is equivalent to its orbital period, both of which are 16.76 local days in duration. Getsi raises moderate, though appreciable, tides on Tleteuāts. When viewed from the surface of Tleteuāts, the apparent size of the moon is about the exact same size than the apparent size of the parent star, Ēnlū and 89% times bigger then earth’s moon. This allows Tleteuāts to experience both total and annular solar eclipses. At full moon, Getsi is 3.28 times as bright as earth’s moon.

Despite its barren appearance, Klenūtsē is shrouded in a diverse atmosphere composed primarily of nitrogen, oxygen, and argon, along with trace gases.


This oxygen is likely produced through various non-biological processes such as photodissociation of water molecules, volcanic outgassing, chemical reactions, and interactions with radiation. These processes contribute to the presence of oxygen in the atmosphere even in the absence of life. With an axial tilt of 12.0 degrees, Klenūtsē experiences noticeable seasonal changes, although its lack of life


means these variations have little impact on the landscape. The planet's atmosphere, with a pressure ranging from 1.2 to 1.5 bars, creates a stable environment suitable for geological processes to continue shaping its surface.

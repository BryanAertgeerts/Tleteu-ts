Overview

The atmosphere of the planet has undergone profound changes over billions of years, evolving through distinct stages shaped by complex interactions of volcanic activity, biological processes, and geological transformations. Each stage reflects shifts in composition, pressure, and temperature, ultimately creating an environment capable of sustaining life.

Timeline of Atmospheric Stages

Stage 1: Initial Atmosphere

In the earliest days of the planet’s formation, the atmosphere was dominated by hydrogen (H₂) and helium (He), gases collected from the cosmic cloud from which the planet was born. However, with extremely high temperatures exceeding 100°C, these light gases quickly escaped into space. The planet’s lower gravity, compared to Earth, further limited its ability to retain these gases. The resulting atmosphere was thin, with pressures under 0.1 bars.

Type: Primary Atmosphere

Composition: Mostly hydrogen (H₂) and helium (He)

Temperature: Over 100°C

Pressure: Very thin, under 0.1 bars

Stage 2: Development of the Secondary Atmosphere

Over the next 10 to 100 million years, volcanic activity reshaped the atmosphere. Intense outgassing released heavier gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂), water vapor (H₂O), and nitrogen (N₂), forming a new type of atmosphere. Trace amounts of methane (CH₄) and ammonia (NH₃) also emerged from the volcanic emissions. As the greenhouse effect took hold, aided by the planet’s close proximity to the Sun, temperatures remained high, stabilizing between 70°C and 100°C. The atmospheric pressure gradually increased to around 0.3–0.5 bars.

Type: Volcanic Outgassing

Composition: CO₂, H₂O vapor, nitrogen (N₂), traces of methane (CH₄) and ammonia (NH₃)

Temperature: 70°C - 100°C

Pressure: 0.3 - 0.5 bars

Stage 3: The Cooling Phase and Formation of Oceans

After roughly 100 million years, the planet began to cool, and water vapor condensed, leading to frequent rainfall and the formation of shallow oceans. As CO₂ dissolved into the oceans, it formed carbonates, reducing the amount of CO₂ in the atmosphere and stabilizing nitrogen levels. This shift moderated temperatures further, cooling the atmosphere to between 50°C and 70°C and establishing a more stable pressure around 0.6 bars. Nitrogen became the dominant gas, while traces of methane and ammonia persisted.

Type: Formation of Oceans

Composition: Reduced CO₂, increased N₂, trace CH₄ and NH₃

Temperature: 50°C - 70°C

Pressure: ~0.6 bars

Stage 4: The Rise of Oxygen

Between 0.5 to 1 billion years into the planet’s history, the emergence of photosynthetic organisms transformed the atmosphere yet again. These life forms gradually produced oxygen (O₂), which accumulated in the atmosphere, while CO₂ levels declined as it was converted by biological processes. This increase in oxygen, combined with decreasing greenhouse gases, led to a cooler and more stable climate, with temperatures ranging from 30°C to 50°C.

Type: Introduction of Oxygen

Composition: Higher N₂ and O₂, with low CO₂

Temperature: 30°C - 50°C

Pressure: Stable, slight increase

Stage 5: A Stabilized Atmosphere

Over the next 2 to 4 billion years, the atmosphere continued to stabilize. Nitrogen (N₂) became the primary gas, with moderate amounts of oxygen (O₂) and only trace amounts of CO₂, creating a balance that could support complex ecosystems. With stable pressure around 0.8 bars and temperatures that ranged from -10°C to 30°C, this mature atmosphere provided the conditions necessary for a diverse array of life forms to thrive.

Type: Modern-Like Atmosphere

Composition: N₂, moderate O₂, trace CO₂

Temperature: -10°C to 30°C

Pressure: ~0.8 bars

Sky Color

the sky exhibits distinct colors throughout the day. At noon, when the star is at its zenith, the sky displays a gray-bluish color, creating a calm and cool atmosphere. As the day transitions into morning and evening, the sky shifts to a deep, dark gray, fading into warmer yellow and orange hues near the horizon. These color changes reflect the unique atmospheric conditions of the planet, making its sky a fascinating subject for study.
